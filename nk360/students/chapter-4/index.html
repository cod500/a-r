Chapter Four

Invasion

Lesson Plan

Guiding Question

How did the Haudenosaunee persevere through American invasions?

Introduction

By 1778, both American colonists and Native people were engaged in a cycle of violent revenge, with raids launched by both sides. Mohawk and Seneca warriors, along with British soldiers, retaliated for previous attacks by launching raids on American settlements near Haudenosaunee territory. In response, General George Washington planned attacks on the Haudenosaunee. In 1779, Washington ordered several of his generals to destroy Haudenosaunee towns, crops, and livestock. They destroyed over forty Haudenosaunee towns. These became known as the Sullivan and Clinton invasions. 

American generals led troops through Haudenosaunee towns, burning everything to the ground. Women, as caretakers and growers of crops, are closely associated with the land in Haudenosaunee culture. Attacking their lands and their crops was an attack on women and their way of life. Thousands of Haudenosaunee people fled for their lives, trying to save what they could, including seeds to replant. They went to British-held Fort Niagara for supplies, but the British did not have enough supplies to help them. The winter was especially difficult, and many Haudenosaunee men, women, children, and elders died from starvation, disease, and cold. When spring finally arrived, many of the Haudenosaunee refugees either returned to their homes to rebuild or moved elsewhere. One of those locations was Buffalo Creek, where some started over and re-established their lifeways and government.

Original Document

“Sir, the expedition you are appointed to command is to be directed against the hostile tribes of the six nations of Indians, with their associates and adherents. The immediate objects are the total destruction and devastation of their settlements and the capture of as many prisoners of every age and sex as possible. It will be essential to ruin the crops now in the ground and prevent their planting more . . .”

“From George Washington to Major General John Sullivan, 31 May 1779,” in The Papers of George Washington, Revolutionary War Series, ed. Edward G. Lengel, vol. 20, 8 April–31 May 1779 (Charlottesville: University of Virginia Press, 2010), 716–719.

These are General George Washington’s orders to General John Sullivan, who led one of the invasions against Haudenosaunee towns and villages.

Document B

Document
Paraphrased Document

Original Document

“The town was beautifully situated . . . where the most extensive fields of corn were, and every kind of vegetable that can be conceived. The whole army was immediately engaged in destroying the crops. The corn was collected and burned in houses and kilns, that the enemy might not reap the least advantage from it . . . I am well persuaded, that, except one . . . there is not a single town left in the country of the five nations.”

“Major General John Sullivan to George Washington, 28 September 1779,” in Papers of George Washington, Revolutionary War Series, ed. Benjamin L. Huggins, vol. 22, 1 August–21 October 1779 (Charlottesville: University of Virginia, 2013), 528–541.

Document D

Document
Paraphrased Document

Original Document

“Many refugees settled at Buffalo Creek in the immediate aftermath of the invasion, where they formed a multinational community near the eastern terminus of Lake Erie that soon became the new Haudenosaunee political center. By the time the war came to a close in 1783, Buffalo Creek was a cosmopolitan community where members of three of the six Haudenosaunee nations (Seneca, Onondaga, and Cayuga) made their home. It was the site of councils regarding internal affairs, where the people worked to reconstitute their damaged confederacy. Additionally, Buffalo Creek was a place where traders, diplomats, and other visitors from Indian nations and colonial governments were welcomed.”

Alyssa Mt. Pleasant, “Independence for Whom?: Expansion and Conflict in the Northeast and Northwest,” in The World of the Revolutionary American Republic: Land, Labor, and the Conflict for a Continent, ed. Andrew Shankman (New York: Routledge, 2014), 125.

Paraphrased Document

Many of the people who ran from the Sullivan invasion moved to Buffalo Creek. Their community was made up of several Native nations and was located near the eastern end of Lake Erie. It soon became an important Haudenosaunee political center. By 1783, people from many nations and cultures lived there and shared their cultures, including Seneca, Onondaga, and Cayuga. They held meetings to make important decisions. They worked to rebuild their Confederacy. It was also a place that welcomed people who traded and worked in politics, as well as visitors from other Native nations and American colonies.

Review the reference timeline that briefly summarizes some of the significant events of this chapter. Use the reference timeline and your work from the document analysis section to complete the chapter review. The chapter review will help you keep track of what you have learned to later use in response to the central question of the digital inquiry book: In what ways did the Haudenosaunee persevere through and after the American Revolution?

April 16, 1779

An American general leads an army invasion through Mohawk and Onondaga territories, destroying homes and crops and forcing Haudenosaunee people to run for their lives. 

June–October 1779

Under General Washington’s orders, American generals invade Haudenosaunee territory with the goal of destroying crops, killing animals, and capturing as many people as they can.

Spring 1780

After the winter thaw, many Haudenosaunee people return to their homes to rebuild. A multicultural community is established at Buffalo Creek, which becomes a thriving town by 1783.

May 1779

General George Washington orders the destruction of Haudenosaunee towns and crops partly in response to British attacks, aided by Senecas and Mohawks, against American settlements.

November 1779

After fleeing for their lives, Haudenosaunee people try to survive the harshest winter on record at Fort Niagara, where there is not enough room or supplies. Many lives are lost.
