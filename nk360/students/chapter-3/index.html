Chapter Three

Tensions Rise

Lesson Plan

Guiding Question

Why did some of the Haudenosaunee align with different sides in the conflict?

Introduction

By 1777, both the British government and American colonists wanted Haudenosaunee support. Haudenosaunee people knew that battles between the British and the American colonists would likely take place on their homelands. In 1776, the Haudenosaunee Grand Council declared neutrality in the war. The Grand Council is and was the Confederacy’s governing organization and was led by Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) from all Six Nations.

Even though the Grand Council of the Confederacy declared neutrality, under the Great Law of Peace, each person has the agency to make their own choices at the local village level. Citizens and communities decided to join the British or the Americans or to remain neutral based on what they believed was best for their communities. Even within Haudenosaunee nations, people allied with opposing sides.

Meeting of Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) from each nation of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy to deal with issues affecting all the nations in the Confederacy.

Not supporting either side in a disagreement, competition, or war.

A Clan Mother heads each clan, an extended family within a nation. Clan mothers serve for life and are responsible for preserving their peoples’ way of life. This includes making all the major decisions that affect the clan, naming people, ensuring people are fed, supervising ceremonies, selecting the male leaders, and removing a leader if he does not serve the interests of his people.

One of the most important events that shaped the Haudenosaunee was the creation of the Gayanesshagowa, the Great Law of Peace. It guides the Haudenosaunee through all aspects of life. A full telling of this history takes several days. It reveals the ways in which the Peacemaker’s teachings emphasized the power of reason, not force, to assure the three principles of the Great Law: righteousness, justice, and health. The Great Law of Peace provides the Haudenosaunee people with instructions on how to treat others, directs them on how to maintain a democratic society, and expresses how reason must prevail to preserve peace.

Control over one's own choices and actions.

The first governing body (1774–89) of the thirteen colonies, which later became the United States.

Mohawk(Kanyen’kehá:ka)

By 1775, the Mohawk were located the closest to American settlements. Their towns and villages were east of the Stanwix Treaty Line, entirely in the colony of New York, leaving their lands in danger of being taken by American colonists. The Revolution seemed like it might be an opportunity to reclaim their land.

Oneida(Onyota’a:ká:)

The Oneida lived near American settlements. Samuel Kirkland, a respected minister and Revolutionary War supporter who was close with Oneida leaders, shared revolutionary ideas with the Oneida. Some Oneida lived nearby Americans and considered them neighbors.

Tuscarora(Skarù·ręʔ)

The Tuscarora are originally from what is present-day North Carolina. In the early 1700s, a group of Tuscarora fled conflict there. The Oneida helped the Tuscarora join the Five Nations Confederacy, which then became the Six Nations Confederacy. As a result of this relationship, many Oneida and Tuscarora influenced each other when it came to deciding which side to support during the Revolutionary War. When many Oneida joined the Americans, many Tuscarora did also.

Onondaga(Ononda'gega')

The Onondaga are the Keepers of the Central Fire of the Confederacy. The Central Fire is a term used to describe Onondaga as a place where Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) gather to discuss important issues affecting all Six Nations. In the 1770s, they suffered the loss of many of their people due to disease. This made it difficult to continue their ceremonies and lifeways. Many did not want their people to fight and die in a war between the British and Americans.

Cayuga(Gayogo̱hó:nǫˀ)

In the west, the Cayuga saw horrible violence when American colonists invaded nearby Shawnee lands in 1774. The Cayuga witnessed the Shawnee lose thousands of acres of land because of the bloody conflict. The Cayuga lived far from most American settlements, and their interactions with Americans in the 1770s were mostly related to violent conflicts.

Seneca(Onöndowa,ga:a)

The Seneca are known as the Keepers of the Western Door because they are located the farthest west of the Six Nations. As such, they maintain Haudenosaunee relationships with other western Native nations. They were affected by American violence against their neighbors, the Shawnee. They were also close to British-controlled Fort Niagara, which the British used to maintain friendly relationships and trade with the Seneca and Cayuga.

(Ganakda'gwennioh) A place where the Haudenosaunee Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) meet in a Grand Council.

The Proclamation of 1763 was supposed to stop colonists from settling west of the Appalachian Mountains on Native lands, but it did not. The 1768 Treaty of Fort Stanwix, between the Haudenosaunee and British, was written to prevent conflict over this land. The Haudenosaunee ceded land to the British, and the British redrew the boundary between the Haudenosaunee and colonists.

Document Analysis

The documents illustrate some of the reasons why Haudenosaunee people joined a side as the conflict intensified. The quotes were recorded by either eyewitnesses or people who later remembered the events.

Document A

Document
Paraphrased Document

Original Document

“. . . if we should lie down and sleep, we should be liable to [have our throat] cut by the Red Coat man [British] or By America if we standby, I therefore say: Take up the offer by the Red Coat man [British], for I shall urge my people to go and join the father British and help his people and his government.”

“A Spring Council at Oswego,” in Chainbreaker: The Revolutionary War Memoirs of Governor Blacksnake as told to Benjamin Williams, ed. Thomas S. Abler (Lincoln: University of Nebraska Press, 1989), 76.

Thayendanegea (also known as Joseph Brant) was a Mohawk warrior closely connected to the British government. His sister, Konwatsi'tsiaienni(also known as Molly or Mary Brant), was married to a high-ranking British official. After visiting Britain to learn more about the conflict in 1776, he believed that the British would protect Haudenosaunee interests better than the Americans. Though Thayendanegea (Joseph Brant) had no formal leadership position among the Mohawk, he was a skilled warrior who spoke several languages and had many connections. He was very influential. He fought in the British army and hoped to convince others to join him. The following quote was recorded in the 1840s by an American author in interviews with a Seneca war chief who remembered the speech.

Paraphrased Document

The Americans went against British orders. They need to be corrected. If you side with the Americans, you will lose your land. Support the British and they will give you everything you want. Your children and women will have enough clothing and food during the war if you side with the British.

. . . if we should lie down and sleep, we should be liable to [have our throat] cut by the Red Coat man [British] or By America if we standby, I therefore say: Take up the offer by the Red Coat man [British], for I shall urge my people to go and join the father British and help his people and his government.

“A Spring Council at Oswego,” in Chainbreaker: The Revolutionary War Memoirs of Governor Blacksnake as told to Benjamin Williams, ed. Thomas S. Abler (Lincoln: University of Nebraska Press, 1989), 76.

Paraphrased Document

If we don’t take a side and we do nothing, it’s likely that either the British or the Americans will attack us. So, join the alliance offered by the British. I tell my people, the Mohawk, to join the British king and help his people and government.

(figurative) A kinship term used in diplomacy to indicate a protective relationship. Haudenosaunee society is matrilineal, meaning that clan lineage is passed through the mother. Children belong to their mother’s clan. The most important male figure in a child’s life would be the mother’s brother. Document B

Document
Paraphrased Document

Original Document

“The . . . declarations of the King that . . . Americans, with all who joined them, would be reduced to wretchedness, had no effect [on] my nation. And on the other hand, [the King’s] promises of a rich reward, on condition of our [supporting him], did not [sway us]; but the love of peace, and the love of our land which gave us birth, supported our resolution [to support the Americans].”

Timothy Pickering, “Good Peter’s Narrative of Several Transactions Respecting Indians Lands,” in Timothy Pickering Papers (Boston: Massachusetts Historical Society), 60:121.

Lakwilotákwas(Good Peter) was an Oneida warrior chief who fought in the Revolution with Americans. In 1792, he spoke with an American official when Haudenosaunee leaders visited the United States government in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. The American official recorded the conversations, likely translated by an American minister. In the statement below, Good Peter describes the Oneida’s decision to support the American colonists in the Revolution.

The . . . declarations of the King that . . . Americans, with all who joined them, would be reduced to wretchedness, had no effect [on] my nation. And on the other hand, [the King’s] promises of a rich reward, on condition of our [supporting him], did not [sway us]; but the love of peace, and the love of our land which gave us birth, supported our resolution [to support the Americans]. 

Timothy Pickering, “Good Peter’s Narrative of Several Transactions Respecting Indians Lands,” in Timothy Pickering Papers (Boston: Massachusetts Historical Society), 60:121.

Paraphrased Document

The king’s threats that the Americans and their allies would be destroyed didn’t convince my nation, the Oneida, to join them. Even when the King offered us rewards for joining the British, we weren’t convinced. It was the love of peace and the love of our homelands that convinced us to support the Americans.

Document C

Document
Paraphrased Document

Original Document

“Previous to the battle of Fort Stanwix, the British sent for the Indians [Seneca and Mohawks] to come and see them whip the rebels; and at the same time stated that they did not wish to have them fight, but wanted to have them just sit down, smoke their pipes, and look on. Our Indians went, to a man; but contrary to their expectation, instead of smoking and looking on, they were obliged to fight for their lives; and in the end of the battle were completely beaten, with a great loss in killed and wounded. Our Indians alone had thirty-six killed, and a great number wounded.”

James E. Seaver, A Narrative of the Life of Mary Jemison (Canandaigua: J. D. Bemis and Co. 1824), 116–117.

Seneca woman Mary Jemison’s biography was recorded by an American historian. Jemison was taken captive as a teenager and later adopted into a Seneca family, with whom she chose to remain for the rest of her life. Americans were interested in her story. In the biography, she describes an incident during which Seneca and Mohawk warriors, who had been neutral, were forced into the Revolutionary conflict.  

Previous to the battle of Fort Stanwix, the British sent for the Indians [Seneca and Mohawks] to come and see them whip the rebels; and at the same time stated that they did not wish to have them fight, but wanted to have them just sit down, smoke their pipes, and look on. Our Indians went, to a man; but contrary to their expectation, instead of smoking and looking on, they were obliged to fight for their lives; and in the end of the battle were completely beaten, with a great loss in killed and wounded. Our Indians alone had thirty-six killed, and a great number wounded.

James E. Seaver, A Narrative of the Life of Mary Jemison (Canandaigua: J. D. Bemis and Co. 1824), 116–117.

Paraphrased Document

Before the battle of Fort Stanwix, the British told the Seneca and Mohawk to come watch them beat the Americans in a battle. The British told the Haudenosaunee they wouldn’t have to fight; they could just watch. The men went, but instead of smoking pipes and watching the conflict, they were drawn into the battle. They had to fight for their lives. In the end, they were completely beaten. Many were killed and wounded. The Senecas lost thirty-six warriors, and many were wounded.
