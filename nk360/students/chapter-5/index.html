Chapter Five

The Practice of Peace

Lesson Plan

Guiding Question

What were the effects of the American Revolution on the Haudenosaunee?

Video

A Clan Mother heads each clan, an extended family within a nation. Clan mothers serve for life and are responsible for preserving their peoples’ way of life. This includes making all the major decisions that affect the clan, naming people, ensuring people are fed, supervising ceremonies, selecting the male leaders, and removing a leader if he does not serve the interests of his people.

Meeting of Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) from each nation of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy to deal with issues affecting all the nations in the Confederacy.

An alliance that unites nations to create a peaceful way of making decisions.

One of the most important events that shaped the Haudenosaunee was the creation of the Gayanesshagowa, the Great Law of Peace. It guides the Haudenosaunee through all aspects of life. A full telling of this history takes several days. It reveals the ways in which the Peacemaker’s teachings emphasized the power of reason, not force, to assure the three principles of the Great Law: righteousness, justice, and health. The Great Law of Peace provides the Haudenosaunee people with instructions on how to treat others, directs them on how to maintain a democratic society, and expresses how reason must prevail to preserve peace.

The American Revolution and the years that followed presented many challenges for the Haudenosaunee. Each community had to make difficult decisions and respond to the effects of the conflict on their people.

Video Vocabulary

In order of video appearance:

  • Neutral: not supporting either side in a disagreement, competition, or war.

  • Central Fire / Council Fire: (Ganakda'gwennioh) a place where the Haudenosaunee Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) meet in a Grand Council.
  • Treaties: formal agreements between two or more nations.
  • Petitioned: to ask (a person, group, or organization) for something in a formal way.
  • Reciprocity: a mutual exchange or dependent relationship that maintains harmony and balance through a shared sense of responsibility, care, and respect for what is given and what is received.
  • Reconciliation: the restoration or process of rebuilding trust, balance, peace, harmony, and connection after a period of conflict.
  • Ceded: to give somebody control of something.
  • Sovereignty: an independent government’s right to determine what happens for its own nation.

Reading: After the Revolution

Individuals and groups within the Haudenosaunee Confederacy fought on opposing sides in the Revolution, but they were still a Confederacy united under the Great Law of Peace. They rekindled the Central Fire after the war. This meant that they reunified and resumed the democratic functions of the Grand Council.

The war between the British and Americans ended in 1783. Even though there were Haudenosaunee on both sides, they were left out of the Treaty of Paris, ending the war between the British and Americans. Not only were the Haudenosaunee excluded from the negotiations, but the British also signed over Haudenosaunee land to the Americans. This was a huge shock to the Haudenosaunee, who had never agreed to give up land.

Without support from their former British allies, the Seneca, Cayuga, Onondaga, and Mohawk were forced to cede a great deal of land, particularly Seneca land, in the Treaty of Fort Stanwix in 1784. As allies of the United States, the Oneida and Tuscarora did not cede any land. Many Haudenosaunee were angry that the United States had negotiated with the nations separately, and not as a Confederacy. Additionally, they argued that the Haudenosaunee representatives who signed the treaty did not have the authority to do so on behalf of the Confederacy. The treaty said that Americans were not supposed to move onto remaining Haudenosaunee lands, but they did anyway. And so, Haudenosaunee people continued to fight to protect their lands from Americans settlement.

By 1794, the Haudenosaunee pressured the Americans to negotiate a new treaty. The Treaty of Canandaigua recognized the nation-to-nation relationship between the two sovereign governments. Both sides wanted peace and to protect their people. The United States gave back more than a million acres taken from the Seneca at the reaty of Fort Stanwix. The Haudenosaunee ceded all land claims to Pennsylvania and the Ohio Valley. In exchange, the United States gained a friendship with the influential Haudenosaunee. The Haudenosaunee received an annual payment of goods. Today, the U.S. government delivers treaty cloth to the Confederacy, which represents the continuing government-to-government relationship between the U.S. and the Haudenosaunee. The Haudenosaunee commemorate the signing of the treaty every year on November 11 to remind the signers that the treaty is the supreme law of the land.

In the 250 years since the American Revolution, the governments of the United States and New York State have both taken land that the Treaty of Canandaigua guaranteed to the Haudenosaunee forever. Many Haudenosaunee now live on reservations that are small compared to their original homelands, but they are still here and continue their government systems. Though the Haudenosaunee Confederacy crosses the United States-Canada border, the governments of the Six Nations continue to meet and discuss issues affecting all of their nations.
