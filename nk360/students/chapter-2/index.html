Chapter Two

War Comes to the Haudenosaunee

Lesson Plan

Guiding Question

Why did the Haudenosaunee Confederacy want to remain neutral early in the American Revolution?

Introduction

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy had long-established relationships with Europeans through diplomacy and trade. These relationships became complicated when the American colonies revolted against the British government.

The practice of building and keeping relationships through discussion, negotiation, and mutual respect.

Map with names of colonial settlements

Map with establishment years of colonial settlements

These two maps show Haudenosaunee homelands in the 1770s. Note how close the British and American settlements are in relation to Haudenosaunee land. Colonists wanted that land for themselves and moved steadily closer to Haudenosaunee towns.

  1. The Royal Proclamation of 1763, Courtesy Library and Archives of Canada

    The Royal Proclamation of 1763, Courtesy Library and Archives of Canada

    October 7, 1763

    After Britain wins the French and Indian War, King George III orders that no colonists should settle west of the Proclamation Line, located along the Appalachian Mountains. The order is supposed to prevent conflict caused by colonists moving onto Native land, but the British government does not enforce the proclamation.

  2. The Stamp Act

    March 22, 1765

    The British government passes the Stamp Act to raise funds by putting a tax on colonial documents.

  3. The Boston Massacre

    March 5, 1770

    A street conflict that results in British soldiers killing several colonists.

  4. The Boston Tea Party

    December 16, 1773

    Colonial protesters throw tea from a trade ship into the sea to protest increased taxation by the British government.

  5. John Kahionhes Fadden (Akwesasne Mohawk, 1938–2022). Tree of Peace. Black and white drawing. Courtesy David Kanietakeron Fadden

    Haudenosaunee leaders declare neutrality

    1774–1776

    Over the course of at least nine councils between 1774 and 1776, during which both the British government and American colonists each try to convince the Haudenosaunee to support their side, the Grand Council of the Haudenosaunee declare themselves friends of both sides, determining it is in their best interests to stay out of the conflict. This is decided repeatedly after careful consideration by Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs), Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) and other leaders.

  6. The First Continental Congress

    September 5, 1774

    Delegates from each colonial state meet in Philadelphia in response to the Intolerable Acts imposed by Britain.

  7. The Battles of Lexington and Concord

    April 9, 1775

    The first major military engagements of the Revolutionary War between American militia and the British military.

  8. John Trumbull (1756–1843). The Declaration of Independence, 1818. Oil on canvas. 3.7 x 5.5 m. Architect of the Capitol

    The Second Continental Congress creates an Indian Department

    June 16, 1775

    One of the early acts of the Continental Congress creates an Indian Department to improve relations between the new American government and Native nations. American leaders know that they need strong relationships with powerful and influential Native nations if their government is going to survive.

  9. The Battle of Bunker Hill

    June 17, 1775

    British troops attack a colonial force in Boston, Massachusetts. The British win the battle but suffer heavy losses. Despite losing, colonial soldiers’ persistence gave many confidence in the American army.

  10. Gilbert Stuart (1755–1828) (possibly). Thayendanegea otherwise Joseph Brant War Chief of the Mohawks, ca. 1785. Oil on canvas. 85 x 72.5 cm. © The Trustees of the British Museum

    Thayendanegea (Joseph Brant), Mohawk leader, visits Britain

    November 11, 1775

    Thayendanegea (Joseph Brant) a Mohawk leader and brother-in-law to the British Superintendent of Indian Affairs travels to England to meet with British leadership and better understand the growing conflict between Britain and the Americans. He wants to make an informed decision about whether to support the British or Americans in the conflict.

  11. John Kahionhes Fadden (Akwesasne Mohawk, 1938–2022). Independence Hall. Black and white drawing. Courtesy David Kanietakeron Fadden

    Haudenosaunee delegation visits Continental Congress

    June 11, 1776

    A group of Haudenosaunee leaders travel to Philadelphia to speak to the Continental Congress. Congressional leaders address them as brothers and hope for continued friendship with the Haudenosaunee.

  12. John Trumbull (1756–1843). The Declaration of Independence, 1818. Oil on canvas. 3.7 x 5.5 m. Architect of the Capitol

    Highlighted text in the Declaration of Independence, Courtesy Mark Charles

    Declaration of Independence

    July 4, 1776

    Continental Congress delegates sign the Declaration, officially proclaiming that the United States is an independent country from Britain. In the document’s grievances about the king, Thomas Jefferson writes that the king of England was encouraging the “merciless Indian Savages” to attack American colonists, even though colonists were invading Native land.

Document Analysis

When conflict between the American colonists and British increased, both sides wanted something from the Haudenosaunee. The Haudenosaunee were considered a powerful and influential Confederacy by the British and Americans. The British wanted Haudenosaunee warriors to join their cause and fight alongside them as many had done in previous wars. The Americans, who could not afford supplies for Haudenosaunee warriors, hoped they would stay out of the conflict. Each of the quotes shows a point of view about Haudenosaunee involvement in the growing Revolutionary conflict.

An alliance that unites nations to create a peaceful way of making decisions.

Të:wëö:nya (Governor Blacksnake) was a Seneca hahsënöwa:neh (civil chief) who told his people’s history to historian Benjamin Williams in the 1840s. He recalled the following speech given by British officials to Haudenosaunee leaders. The speech was given at one of several councils the Haudenosaunee had in 1777 to address the growing Revolutionary conflict.

Paraphrased Document

The Americans went against British orders. They need to be corrected. If you side with the Americans, you will lose your land. Support the British and they will give you everything you want. Your children and women will have enough clothing and food during the war if you side with the British.

Document B

Document
Paraphrased Document

Original Document

“This quarrel does not belong to us, and it is best for us to take no part in it; we need not waste our blood to have it settled. If they fight us, we will fight them, but if they leave us alone, we better keep still.”

John Niles Hubbard, An Account of Sa-go-ye-wat-ha, or Red Jacket and His People, 1750–1830 (Albany, N.Y.: Joel Munsell’s Sons, 1886), 45

William Jones, a Seneca man who was born after the Revolution, spoke to American author John Niles Hubbard about Seneca history. In 1886, Jones recounted the story of Seneca diplomat Sagoyewatha (Red Jacket) who lived during the Revolution. Hubbard recorded what Jones said in An Account of Sa-go-ye-wat-ha.

Early in the war, Red Jacket warned his fellow Seneca about getting involved in the Revolutionary conflict. He spoke at a Grand Council meeting at Onondaga where Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs), Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) and community leaders met to discuss issues affecting the Confederacy. At the time of the speech, both the Americans and British were pressuring the Haudenosaunee for their friendship in the conflict. Following is part of Red Jacket’s remarks:

This quarrel does not belong to us, and it is best for us to take no part in it; we need not waste our blood to have it settled. If they fight us, we will fight them, but if they leave us alone, we better keep still.

John Niles Hubbard, An Account of Sa-go-ye-wat-ha, or Red Jacket and His People, 1750–1830 (Albany, N.Y.: Joel Munsell’s Sons, 1886), 45

Paraphrased Document

The fight between the British and Americans has nothing to do with us, so we shouldn’t take a side. We shouldn’t fight and die to help them settle it. If they attack us, we will fight back; but if they leave us alone, then we should stay neutral.

A person whose job is to represent their country in a foreign country.

Meeting of Hodiya'neh'son (chiefs) and Gayeñ nehda' Gondi'wehnyoñ (clan mothers) from each nation of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy to deal with issues affecting all the nations in the Confederacy.

A Clan Mother heads each clan, an extended family within a nation. Clan mothers serve for life and are responsible for preserving their peoples’ way of life. This includes making all the major decisions that affect the clan, naming people, ensuring people are fed, supervising ceremonies, selecting the male leaders, and removing a leader if he does not serve the interests of his people.

American colonists knew the Haudenosaunee Confederacy could help them win the war against the British. However, the American colonists did not have enough money to buy weapons and supplies for Haudenosaunee warriors. In August 1775, colonial officials made the following argument to Haudenosaunee leaders at one of two conferences in Albany, New York.

Paraphrased Document 

This fight is between American colonists and England, and you are not part of it. We don’t want you to fight the British troops. We want you to stay home, remain neutral, and keep the peace.

We hope to stay friendly with your government for all time.

The next year, Haudenosaunee leaders visited the Continental Congress. American leaders again asked the Haudenosaunee for their friendship. As was common practice when meeting with the Haudenosaunee, American representatives followed Haudenosaunee practices for discussion. The Americans stated:

We hope that the friendship that is between us and you will be firm, and continue as long as the sun shall shine . . .

Journals of the Continental Congress, 1774-1789, ed. Worthington C. Ford et al. (Washington, DC, 1906), 5:430
