In this digital inquiry book, students will answer the central question:

In what ways did the Haudenosaunee persevere through and after the American Revolution?

By exploring a guiding question in each of the five chapters in this educational resource, middle school students will find evidence to show that while the Haudenosaunee faced significant challenges during and after the Revolution, they persevered by making a series of difficult decisions to preserve their government and lifeways, protect their communities, and maintain their sovereignty.

Instructional Method

A digital inquiry book poses a central question to students. Each chapter provides a multimedia experience framed by a guiding question for students using primary and secondary sources, maps, videos, and images. Students will use the sources to help them build an evidence-based response to the central question.

Rationale for Inquiry

Many textbooks’ coverage of the American Revolution focuses on the experiences of colonial and British participants. This coverage excludes the perspectives of thousands of Indigenous peoples who were directly affected by both the violence of the war and the resulting policies of the newly created United States.


The experiences of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy, a union of six nations whose homelands are in what is now New York State and beyond, are important for students to investigate because:

  • The American Revolution was fought on, and for, Haudenosaunee homelands that shared a border with colonial settlements. This put the Haudenosaunee Confederacy at the very center of the conflict, whether they wanted to participate or not.
  • The Haudenosaunee Confederacy's democratic values and balanced power structure influenced the governmental structure of the United States.
  • The Haudenosaunee are the longest continuously running democracy in the Western Hemisphere.

Recommended Prior Student Knowledge

Students should have a basic understanding of the rising tensions between the British government and the American colonies in the mid- to late eighteenth century. They should also have a general understanding that in the eighteenth century, most of the North American continent was populated by diverse Indigenous nations who had sophisticated governments and lifeways with advanced diplomatic and trade networks. Indigenous peoples who traded with Europeans in the eighteenth century had a keen awareness of the conflicts between European powers and carefully considered ways in which they could use these conflicts to their advantage. The primary concern of most Indigenous communities was doing what was best for their own communities in any international conflict.

Haudenosaunee Languages

Language is essential to culture and identity. Each of the Six Nations has its own language that has been passed down from generation to generation and has evolved over time. We have included Haudenosaunee words and names in this resource so that students can learn Haudenosaunee words from Haudenosaunee speakers. There are various ways to spell each of the words we have included; these are not 'standardized’ spellings. They are spellings provided to us by speakers of each language. If you are interested in the orthography of Haudenosaunee languages, we encourage you to reach out to Haudenosaunee language institutes and schools. We have also included pronunciations so students can hear how the words sound. Haudenosaunee languages are polysynthetic, meaning that several morphemes are combined to create a word. The words are not necessarily phonetic, and so it is important that students listen to the pronunciations but also practice them.

Academic Standards

In an effort to provide skills-based assessments and to meet the needs of a diverse body of teachers, we have elected to use two nationally recognized sets of skills-based standards: the Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts and the College, Career, and Civic Life (C3) Framework for Social Studies State Standards. The museum recognizes the expertise of educators to further align the components of this resource to the academic standards specific to their state. New York state standards are included because the Haudenosaunee, who are featured in this inquiry, have lived on the lands that are now New York State for generations.

Essential Understandings

Building on the ten themes of the National Council for the Social Studies' national curriculum standards, the National Museum of the American Indian's Essential Understandings reveal key concepts about the rich and diverse cultures, histories, and contemporary lives of Native peoples. These concepts reflect a multitude of untold stories about American Indians that can deepen and expand your teaching of history, geography, civics, economics, science, engineering, and other subject areas.


This resource addresses the following Essential Understandings.

1: American Indian Cultures

This resource addresses the following Key Concepts from Essential Understanding 1: American Indian Cultures:

  • American Indians are both individuals and members of a tribal group.
  • American Indian cultures have always been dynamic and changing.

  • Native people continue to fight to maintain the integrity and viability of indigenous societies. American Indian history is one of cultural persistence, creative adaptation, renewal, and resilience.

2: Time, Continuity, and Change

This resource addresses the following Key Concepts from Essential Understanding 2: Time, Continuity, and Change:

  • American Indian history is not singular or timeless. American Indian cultures have always adapted and changed in response to environmental, economic, social, and other factors. American Indian cultures and people are fully engaged in the modern world.
  • Hearing and understanding American Indian history from Indian perspectives provides an important point of view to the discussion of history and cultures in the Americas. Indian perspectives expand the social, political, and economic dialogue.

  • Indigenous people played a significant role in the history of the Americas. Many of these historically important events and developments in the Americas shaped the modern world.

3: People, Places, and Environments

This resource addresses the following Key Concept from Essential Understanding 3: People, Places, and Environments:

  • The imposition of international, state, reservation, and other borders on Native lands changed relationships between people and their environments, affected how people lived, and sometimes isolated tribal citizens and family members from one another.

5: Individuals, Groups, and Institutions

This resource addresses the following Key Concept from Essential Understanding 5: Individuals, Groups, and Institutions:

  • Today, because of treaties, court decisions, and statutes, tribal governments maintain a unique relationship with federal and state governments.

6: Power, Authority, and Governance

This resource addresses the following Key Concept from Essential Understanding 6: Power, Authority, and Governance:

  • Long before European colonization, American Indians had developed a variety of complex systems of government that embodied important principles for effective rule. American Indian governments and leaders interacted, recognized each other's sovereignty, practiced diplomacy, built strategic alliances, waged wars, and negotiated peace accords.

7: Production, Distribution, and Consumption

This resource addresses the following Key Concept from Essential Understanding 7: Production, Distribution, and Consumption:

  • American Indians played influential and powerful roles in trade and exchange economies with partners in Europe during the colonial period. These activities also supported the development and growth of the United States.

10: Civic Ideals and Practices

This resource addresses the following Key Concept from Essential Understanding 10: Civic Ideals and Practices:

  • As citizens of their tribal nations, American Indians have always had certain rights, privileges, and responsibilities that are tied to cultural values and beliefs and thus vary from culture to culture.
