Chapter Two

War Comes to the
Haudenosaunee
Lesson Plan

Guiding Question
Why did the Haudenosaunee Confederacy want to remain neutral early in the American Revolution?
Download PDF

At a Glance

Student Objectives

Skills

  • Examine a map using close-looking strategies to determine the relationships between key groups.
  • Interpret timeline evidence to draw conclusions about peacemaking efforts by the Haudenosaunee.
  • Interpret primary source texts and select evidence to identify speakers’ motivations.

Content

  • Understand the increasing tension between the Haudenosaunee and American colonists resulting from increased American settlement on Haudenosaunee lands.
  • Understand the external pressures that led to challenging decisions for Haudenosaunee people at the start of the American Revolution.
  • Understand the reasons why the Haudenosaunee wanted to stay out of the growing Revolutionary conflict.

Teacher Instructions

Activate Prior Knowledge

  1. Introduce students to the Chapter 2 Guiding Question: Why did the Haudenosaunee Confederacy want to remain neutral early in the American Revolution? Engage students in a discussion about neutrality. What does it mean to them? Prompt them for examples of neutrality they might know from history or their own lives. What are some reasons people remain neutral in conflict?

Student Task

Introduction

  1. Read the map introduction paragraph to the class.
  2. Ask students to take a close look at the map, either on individual devices or projected. Ask them to look closely at the details. What do they notice? What information is the map trying to communicate?
Teacher Tip:   For a supported option, this might be a good time to practice close-looking by setting a timer and encouraging students to jot down words or phrases describing what they see. Prompt them to notice the locations of different groups in proximity to each other.
  1. Engage the class in the comprehension questions.
  • What do you notice about Haudenosaunee homelands in relation to British forts and American settlements?
Teacher Tip:   Guide students toward understanding that the British and Americans were on the borders of Haudenosaunee territory.
  • What might the location of British forts and American settlements mean for Haudenosaunee safety in the 1770s?
Teacher Tip:   For a supported option, this might be a good time to practice close looking by setting a timer and encouraging students to jot down words or phrases describing what they see. Prompt them to notice the locations of different groups in proximity to each other.
Start
Introduction

Timeline

  1. Read the timeline introduction and the names of the events out loud to students. Ask students if there are events they already recognize.
  2. Before students read the timeline event descriptions, present the comprehension question: Based on the events in the timeline, what actions did Haudenosaunee people take to maintain peace?
  3. Have students read through the timeline event descriptions as partner pairs or individuals. As a whole class, invite a few students to share their responses to the comprehension question. Record student responses in a visible place for reference in the next chapter.
Teacher Tip:   Guide students to identify the multiple declarations of neutrality between 1774 and 1776, and the Haudenosaunee delegation congressional visit in 1776 as peacekeeping efforts.
Start Timeline

Formative Assessment: Document Analysis

  1. Read the document bank introduction aloud to students. Before moving on to the next section, ask students what about this introduction stands out to them.
Teacher Tip:   To guide students’ thinking, ask: What was the relationship between the Haudenosaunee and Americans and British by the 1770s?
  1. As a class or in partner pairs, have students close-read each document and discuss each comprehension question. Use the Close-Reading Strategy options for differentiation.
  2. After close-reading each document, have students respond to the document analysis questions. They will describe what the speaker wants and identify words in the document that helped them identify the speaker’s reasoning.
Teacher Tip:  
  • Remind students that the paraphrases in the “Paraphrased Document” tab might be useful support tools for this task. While helpful as support tools, the paraphrases should not be used as supporting evidence.
  • A supported option for this task is a jigsaw. Each member of a small group analyzes a different document and shares their analysis with the rest of the group. Alternatively, assign one document to each small group to analyze together. Then, each group presents their responses to the class.
  1. Students will synthesize their responses in answering the Chapter 2 Guiding Question.

Students can complete the task in one of two ways:

Closing

  1. Ask students which actions taken by the Haudenosaunee to maintain neutrality stood out to them.
  2. Instruct students to complete the chapter review using the reference timeline and their responses to the document analysis section. The chapter review will help students keep track of what they have learned in each chapter to later use in the Summative Assessment, similar to notetaking.
  3. Students will list the challenges faced by the Haudenosaunee in each chapter and the acts of perseverance by Haudenosaunee in the face of those challenges.
